## The Thermoacoustic Effect

Sound is a mechanical wave that propagates through the back-and-forth oscillation of a medium. In a gas, an acoustic wave drives a periodic displacement of the fluid together with periodic pressure variation.

Compression and expansion in the cycle also drive a temperature oscillation, in step with the displacement. When a low-frequency wave passes through a gas confined within sufficiently small channels — for example inside a regenerator or a stack — the oscillating gas exchanges heat with the channel walls because of the temperature difference between them, giving rise to the rich set of phenomena known collectively as **thermoacoustic effects**.

The earliest written record of a thermoacoustic phenomenon appears in 16th-century Japan: the *Tamonin Nikki* (《多聞院日記》, 1568) — a diary kept at Kofuku-ji temple in Nara — describes the <a href="https://youtu.be/-lYBRUHMJG4?t=410" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">"narukama" (鳴釜) ritual</a> at Kibitsu Shrine in Bicchū (present-day Okayama), in which a sacred kettle audibly rings during oracular ceremonies. The same phenomenon was later dramatized in the 18th-century supernatural-tale collection *Ugetsu Monogatari* (《雨月物語》, 1776), in the chapter "Kibitsu no Kama" (吉備津の釜). The kettle, sketched in Fig. 1(a), is a long iron pot topped with a bamboo steaming basket (*seiro*) on which rice grains are sprinkled; steam from the boiling water heats the small channels formed between the rice grains, exciting a self-sustained acoustic oscillation. In 19th-century Europe, glass blowers occasionally noticed sound emerging from a glass tube when the cold end was pressed against a hot blob of molten glass; Sondhauss made the first systematic experimental investigation of the phenomenon, and the device was thereafter named the *Sondhauss tube* (Fig. 1(b)). Around the same period, Rijke placed a heated wire mesh at the quarter-length point inside a tube open at both ends and likewise observed sound generation — the famous <a href="https://youtu.be/abswNCqnMRQ?t=90" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Rijke tube</a> shown in Fig. 1(c).

In the late 19th and early 20th century, Lord Rayleigh first described the thermoacoustic effect qualitatively in his classic *The Theory of Sound*:

> *If heat be given to the air at the moment of greatest condensation or taken from it at the moment of greatest rarefaction, the vibration is encouraged.*

This sentence captures the essence of the thermoacoustic mechanism.

Figure 2 makes the picture concrete. A row of gas parcels sits inside a channel; the steep positive axial temperature gradient imposed on the channel is shown by the red-to-blue gradient. When a parcel is perturbed, it absorbs heat from the wall at the hot side and releases heat at the cold side. Because heat absorption sustains compression / expansion across the back-and-forth motion, the initial perturbation grows into a stable oscillation.

For this back-and-forth heat exchange to actually drive the cycle, the gas parcel and the channel wall must operate in the right heat-transfer regime. The decisive parameter is the **thermal penetration depth** $\delta_\alpha$ multiplied by the acoustic angular frequency $\omega$ — i.e. $\omega\tau_\alpha$ with $\tau_\alpha = \delta_\alpha^2/\alpha$ the thermal-diffusion timescale. Fig. 4(a) shows the three regimes as cross-sections of the flow channel; Fig. 4(b) shows the same regimes mapped onto the distance from the wall:

The irreversible regime is where the thermoacoustic effect actually lives: neither fully adiabatic (so the wall can transfer heat) nor fully isothermal (so a phase lag remains and pressure can do work). Regenerators and stacks are engineered precisely so that the channel radius $r$ and the operating-frequency $\delta_\alpha$ fall on the same scale.

## Why this matters — from piston-less Stirling engines to thermoacoustic engines

A heat engine converts thermal energy into mechanical work, and may be either an internal- or external-combustion engine.

Among external-combustion engines that run high-efficiency thermodynamic cycles, the Stirling engine is a notable example. An α-type Stirling engine is sketched in Fig. 3(a): two solid pistons reciprocate 90° out of phase to produce work. A thermoacoustic engine is also an external-combustion engine, but unlike a mechanical engine its output is in the form of an oscillating acoustic pressure wave; when energy is converted via the traveling-wave component of the acoustic field, the underlying thermodynamic process closely approximates the Stirling cycle.

As Fig. 3(a) shows, in a looped configuration with a regenerator and hot and cold heat exchangers, a thermoacoustic engine is essentially a Stirling engine with the crank-and-piston assembly stripped away. Figure 3(b) takes a microscopic view of the gas parcels oscillating inside the regenerator: the back-and-forth motion of the two pistons forces a 90° phase difference between pressure and displacement oscillations — equivalently, the parcel's pressure and velocity oscillate *in phase*, which is the signature of a traveling acoustic wave propagating from cold end to hot end. So when a traveling wave like that of Fig. 3(c) passes through a regenerator with an axial temperature gradient, each parcel undergoes a sequence of thermodynamic processes that approximates the Stirling cycle.

Replacing the mechanical piston with an acoustic wave makes a thermoacoustic engine remarkably simple in construction. It largely sidesteps the sliding-seal and mechanical-complexity issues of conventional Stirling engines, beating them on reliability and cost; recent reports place its thermal efficiency on par with internal-combustion engines [1, 2].
